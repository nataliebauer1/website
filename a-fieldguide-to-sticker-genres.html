A Field Guide to Sticker Genres

Introduction

When talking to people about stickers, they always have something in mind - they might have recently gotten a cute one from a friend, picked one up at an animal rights booth, or saw an offensive one in the streets. However, while normally around the size of the palm of the hand, sticker is not just sticker. Most of them cater to a collective identity. This means that they try to communicate something to an audience that is only able to decipher its claims if it is familiar with the visual and linguistic symbolism used. If successfull, the audience has some sort of emotional reaction to the sticker, e.g., in political dis-/agreement or wanting to purchase an item. Additionally, just like any other form of art and communication, stickers cannot be placed strictly into one category; instead certain genres can be observed. To start things off, you will get an introduction to the different types of stickers out there before we move on to topics from related fields, such as street art. To that end, one type of stickers will be featured every tuesday.

The Sports Fan 

07 April 2026

Football (soccer in the US and other places) is the most popular sport worldwide, and especially in Europe (World Population Review, 2026). Accordingly, its popularity has led to intense fan cultures that not only happen around matches, but also bleed into fan club rivalries and territorial conflicts, too. Therefore, they serve as an obvious example when talking about the relation between sports and stickers. Kick-off In general, footie stickers typically appear en route to the next match, e.g., near local train stations, but are also used to establish dominance over other areas. Vigsø (2010) called this behaviour tribal demarcation: prone-to-violence ultras address fellow fans and opponents in their own and by their adversaries claimed neighbourhoods. Papoutzis et al. (2014) describe this phenomenon regarding fans of Aris FC as "a coded, agressive and propagandist discourse of persuasion and domination" (2014, 663). In the latter case, that matter is mostly directed at the fans of the selected opponent POAK. In line with both authors above, Wachendorff (2024) finds in their analysis of football stickers (N=1000), placed in two North Rhine-Westphalian cities in Germany, that football stickers also serve as territorial and group identity marks among fans through loyalty to a club and that stickers of the respective local club dominate the area by far (82% for BVB-Dortmund in Dortmund; 80% Rot-Weiss Essen in Essen; Section 5.3 Football). It has been additionally reported for said cities that placement of stickers diverging from the common opinion can even result in so-called 'home visits', supposedly physical altercations between fans (Wachendorff, 2024). Club colours Wachendorff (2024) and Papoutzis et al. (2014), furthermore, point out that fanmade stickers often carry strong emotional messages of love, passion, and opposition; linking the fan, fan club, and football club without any further subtext to speak of. Besides, the type of lettering, specifically the use of serif typefaces, plays into this affective behaviour, creating "a sense of intimacy like a love letter" (Wachendorff, 2024, Section 5.3 Football). Paputzis et al. (2014) analysed the Super 3 fan clubs', which are affiliated to Aris FC, use of stickers (N=79), which typically consist of the spatial code of the local fan division, the Super 3 logotype and another dominant element like a person or a group of people; an object like a geographically important building to the subdivision; or an animal like the bulldog. Supposedly because of traditional marches before and after games of Aris FC, stickers typically show people (66%), who, in combination with text and other symbols, appeal to the audience to take action against other (fan) clubs. While landmarks highlight the geographic attachment of subdivisions at first glance, they are used to tie local fan groups to the greater organisation as well, feeding into earlier-mentioned ideas of collective identity building (percentage not given). Lastly, in 58% of all cases, the fan clubs used a stylised bulldog that carries symbolism of "the club's strength, pride, determination and bravery and, above all, its dedication and loyalty to the football team" (Papoutzis et al., 2014: 656). Additionally established symbols at Super 3's hands are the club's colours (found on 96% of the database) and its patron, Ares, the god of war. Virtually all stickers are either in Greek, English, or both (17%; 31%; 51%; Papoutzis et al., 2014, 658). Wachendorff’s text also suggests that soccer stickers are primarily manufactured in German and English (2024, Section 3 Stickers in Comparison to Other Signs). Vigsø (2010) solely states that stickers commonly display club colours and the hooligan club's name and logo. They conclude that stickers seem to only appeal to scene insiders, and their main use is a spatial and visual translation of the struggles between hooliganism groups. A recurring theme across this type of literature is that the territorial logic of fan stickers is inherently relational: claims to space that elevate a fan clubs visibility, potentially inviting contestation.

Building on Papoutzis et al.’s (2014), Vigsø’s (2010), and Wachendorff’s (2024) findings on dominance, group identity, and spatial marking, my own data collection from the past two years similarly suggests that sports club fans are among the most active producers and placers of stickers in public space. These stickers frequently appear layered over those of rival fan groups, indicating an ongoing visual struggle over symbolic dominance of a place. At the same time, different fan groups appear to operate on distinct levels of visibility and communication from my observation. While more general fan stickers (either fan-made or officially issued) often rely on club colours, their logos, and broadly affective messages of loyalty, ultras and hooligan circles tend to employ coded references, such as the stadium curve from which they typically watch the match. Such codes function simultaneously as markers of in-group belonging and as instruments of intimidation directed at outsiders, without necessarily addressing them. Important to keep in mind is that even though individuals put these stickers up, the act truly is about belonging to a football (fan) club: neither in the reviewed literature nor in my own data is a sticker that explicitly states personal sentiments, unlike, for instance, a tourist sticker. My own data further tentatively suggests that this form of sticker-based territorial marking is particularly characteristic of European football fan cultures. Hence, it is unclear how cultures with different preferred sports act. Final whistle In conclusion, sports fan stickers function as far more than decorative expressions of club support. As the literature and my own observations suggest, they operate as visible markers of territoriality, collective identity, and emotional affiliation within fan cultures. By claiming public space, often in direct competition with rival groups, these stickers transform everyday spaces into symbolic landscapes. At the same time, their visual language, ranging from official club colours and logos to more coded references used by ultras, reinforces boundaries between insiders and outsiders, often through emotionally charged language.

The Advertisement 

31 March 2026

This one might not be too on the nose: although stickers have been accessible and ready to be mass-produced ever since the invention of the ink-jet printer, they have somewhat failed to be recognised as a standard tool for advertisement, unlike billboards or flyers, likely due to their historical connotation with use and reception by young people and subcultures (Vigsø, 2010). Despite this relative neglect, companies have nonetheless made use of these easily produced and cheap advertising spaces. They typically contain the company’s logo, name, and a means of contact, along with either a commonly associated symbol, a work sample, or a depiction of the finished product. Generally speaking, they are rarely covered up, except, perhaps, by rival companies. While scanning anything you see on the streets is discouraged (after all, a QR code could contain a virus or trojan), cleverly designed and strategically placed stickers are nonetheless a great way for companies to boost brand awareness and spark interest in a product among prospective customers. An example of this is the commonly found placement of roller shutter repair service stickers on garage doors in Singapore, reinforcing the service’s usefulness. At the same time, the way stickers are employed differs across regions. As seen in the example, in South East Asia they are often a straightforward listing of the service, with no frills and a phone number, mostly for handyman services or website design. In Western contexts, such as Europe and Australia, the design is typically more sophisticated and generally aimed at an “alternative” clientele that might be interested in tattoos, start-up or tech services, niche magazines, or queer events.

The Tourist 

24 March 2026

Like a guest who has made themselves at home and now refuses to leave, it clings to some lamppost in some city, brought by someone who has left again already. A stylised instagram handle at a viewpoint somewhere in Queensland, Australia, a fading “Nett hier, aber waren Sie schon mal in Baden-Württemberg?”-sticker1 on a lamp pole bin in Singapore. The tourist sticker leaves a mark, most of the time showing a symbol typical for a traveller's hometown, like a coat of arms, or plainly spelling its name out. While hardly ever displaying authorship, it whispers, “I was here”, even when the person who placed it is long gone. However, thanks to travel influencers and other guides, tourists tend to end up in similar places, leaving many “I was here”s for the next visitor to line up. From experience (and maybe placing a lot of good will in people), they hardly ever get overstickered. Instead, a mural by visitors, who, if not anything else, claimed a tiny part of the place they came to, emerges.

1) "Nice here, but have you ever been in Baden-Württemberg?" is a tourism campaign by the German state of Baden-Württemberg that has enjoyed unbroken popularity among German holidaymakers for years.
