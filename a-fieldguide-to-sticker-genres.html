A Field Guide to Sticker Genres

Introduction

When talking to people about stickers, they always have something in mind - they might have recently gotten a cute one from a friend, picked one up at an animal rights booth, or saw an offensive one in the streets. However, while normally around the size of the palm of the hand, sticker is not just sticker. Most of them cater to a collective identity. This means that they try to communicate something to an audience that is only able to decipher its claims if it is familiar with the visual and linguistic symbolism used. If successfull, the audience has some sort of emotional reaction to the sticker, e.g., in political dis-/agreement or wanting to purchase an item. Additionally, just like any other form of art and communication, stickers cannot be placed strictly into one category; instead certain genres can be observed. To start things off, you will get an introduction to the different types of stickers out there before we move on to topics from related fields, such as street art. To that end, one type of stickers will be featured every tuesday.

The Advertisement 

31 March 2026

This one might not be too on the nose: although stickers have been accessible and ready to be mass-produced ever since the invention of the ink-jet printer, they have somewhat failed to be recognised as a standard tool for advertisement, unlike billboards or flyers, likely due to their historical connotation with use and reception by young people and subcultures (Vigsø, 2010). Despite this relative neglect, companies have nonetheless made use of these easily produced and cheap advertising spaces. They typically contain the company’s logo, name, and a means of contact, along with either a commonly associated symbol, a work sample, or a depiction of the finished product. Generally speaking, they are rarely covered up, except, perhaps, by rival companies. While scanning anything you see on the streets is discouraged (after all, a QR code could contain a virus or trojan), cleverly designed and strategically placed stickers are nonetheless a great way for companies to boost brand awareness and spark interest in a product among prospective customers. An example of this is the commonly found placement of roller shutter repair service stickers on garage doors in Singapore, reinforcing the service’s usefulness. At the same time, the way stickers are employed differs across regions. As seen in the example, in South East Asia they are often a straightforward listing of the service, with no frills and a phone number, mostly for handyman services or website design. In Western contexts, such as Europe and Australia, the design is typically more sophisticated and generally aimed at an “alternative” clientele that might be interested in tattoos, start-up or tech services, niche magazines, or queer events.

The Tourist 

24 March 2026

Like a guest who has made themselves at home and now refuses to leave, it clings to some lamppost in some city, brought by someone who has left again already. A stylised instagram handle at a viewpoint somewhere in Queensland, Australia, a fading “Nett hier, aber waren Sie schon mal in Baden-Württemberg?”-sticker1 on a lamp pole bin in Singapore. The tourist sticker leaves a mark, most of the time showing a symbol typical for a traveller's hometown, like a coat of arms, or plainly spelling its name out. While hardly ever displaying authorship, it whispers, “I was here”, even when the person who placed it is long gone. However, thanks to travel influencers and other guides, tourists tend to end up in similar places, leaving many “I was here”s for the next visitor to line up. From experience (and maybe placing a lot of good will in people), they hardly ever get overstickered. Instead, a mural by visitors, who, if not anything else, claimed a tiny part of the place they came to, emerges.

1) "Nice here, but have you ever been in Baden-Württemberg?" is a tourism campaign by the German state of Baden-Württemberg that has enjoyed unbroken popularity among German holidaymakers for years.
